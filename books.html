    First half is explaining, why the gen Z are the way they are (culprit is screen time and social media). Second part is on preventive measures. Target audience is mainly the parents of young children. It would've being nicer if it's written to more general audience.

  15. Think Faster, Talk Smarter: How to Speak Successfully When You're Put on the Spot by Matt Abrahams (3/5)
    Two parts of the book. First explains the theory and second gives practical advice on specific scenarios. Read the first part and skimmed the second.
  16. The Road to Character by David Brooks (3.5/5)
    Enjoyed the first part of the book. Carries a strong punch and engaging. But the last few chapters are weak. Feels like the author ran out of good stuff and is fishing. The conclusion is also not very convincing.

  2. The Unexpected Joy of the Ordinary by Catherine Gray (3/5)
    I saw a person reading this book on train. And found good reviews online. The book was available in the library@harbourfront. So I went there just to collect this book. Though it's an easy read it's not as enlightening as I expected.

  4. Brave New World by Aldous Huxley (4/5)

    Science stuff and talking to self gets boring after a while. Skimmed through after the first half of the book. Tries a bit too hard to be funny. All the characters are altruistic (even the alien). Only bad guys in the book are single cell creatures.
